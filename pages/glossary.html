Glossary

Glossary

+ Droll
From the Samuel Johnson Dictionary: "One whose business is to raise mirth by petty tricks; a jester; a buffoon" and "a farce; something exhibited to raise mirth"; largely associated with the drolls of the Interregnum in England, acts of guerrilla and underground theatre and performance that kept entertainment alive in a time of extreme artistic suppression. The Drolls were extremely bawdy and crass acts put on spur of the moment by small troupes of actors with little to no set or costumes and very minimal props. Acts were short, often abridged or adapted from previous existing material, folk tales and pastorals, or improvised on the spot by actors.
+ Interregnum and Puritan England
The period of Puritan rule under leader Oliver Cromwell after the beheading of King Charles I and before his son's ascendency to the throne. During and after the English Civil War. The Puritans banned most forms of merriment and entertainment, including theatre, gambling, Christmas and Easter celebrations, dancing and singing in public, rhyming in public, etc. They did allow things like the opera, likely due to Cromwell's personal tastes. The period of theatrical reopenings after this time moved us towards the modern model of smaller indoor theatres and single-purpose private theatrical venues, and away from a long history of outdoor, publicly accessible theatre.
+ Blackamoor
See this article. From the script, "Africans began living in England in the 1400s. Most came from the Barbary coast or countries in Western Africa. At the time of their arrival, Tudor England was a small, relatively weak kingdom which had not yet experienced the horrors of the transatlantic slave trade. The blackamoors inhabited every segment of English society, from the royal court to the bawdy houses."
+ Walls of Jericho
A biblical story about a military battle wone by Israelites over Jericho. The story is allegorically about persistence (continuous regular assault won the battle) and the weight of the promises of God in the bible.
+ Henry Purcell
A famed Baroque English composer, born near the end of the Interregnum. His music is featured in our production.
+ Turnbull Street
A place of ill-repute, a street known for dangerous and colorful characters. This street is a joke in several Elizabethan plays, a known shorthand for a "bad part of town".
+ Magistrate
From the Samuel Johnson Dictionary: "A man publicly invested with authority; a governor; an executor of the laws."
+ Flayed
Stripped of skin.
+ Tuppence, Three-penny, Shillings
One shilling is 12 pennies. A Tuppence is 2 pennies, and Three-penny (or thruppence) is self-descriptive.
+ Bumpkin (archaic)
From the Samuel Johnson Dictionary: "An awkward heavy rustic; a country lout." I encourage the reading of this full entry, it's quite funny.
+ Wantonness
Reckless, unrestrained; lascivious.
+ Dunk thee in the Thames
See Map. This was a real form of punishment, often against women who were too loud, outspoken, or employed in sex work (and caught by the law). A custom "cucking" or "ducking" stool allowed the body to be strapped on, and then swiveled over the water of the Thames and dunked multiple times. A powerful form of public shaming, potentially dangerous, and usually heavily attended by a crowd of jeering spectators (as most punishments and executions were at the time).
+ Purloined
Stolen.
+ Poultice
A medicated paste or clump applied to wounds.
+ Royalist
Aristocrats and others who remained loyal to the monarchy of Charles I (and his lineage) after Cromwell's takeover.
+ The Crown Inn/Tavern
See Map. John and Jane Davenant, parents of William, took ownership over the tavern around 1600 and earned a coveted wine license within the city. Shakespeare lived in or frequented the location, and is rumored to be the real father of William. Shakespeare's documents, left lying around the Tavern, contribute importantly to our contemporary knowledge of him - that is, that we know of him at all is often attributed to the papers kept by William Davenant from the Crown and used in the theatres during the Restoration.
+ Prigger
Rude slang for a thief.
+ Troth
An oath of loyalty; truth.
+ Fart Fire and Brimstone
Farts that smell like sulfur.
+ Swigman
A thief/beggar that pretends to be selling or buying real goods; a conman.
+ Crank
A capricious or overly enthusiastic person.
+ Keech
A lump of fat.
+ Bawd
Madam of a brothel; sex worker.
+ Slut
Dirty woman.
+ (Sotto)
Softly, quietly.
+ Windbag
Talks a lot, says nothing.
+ Prattler
Talks a lot, says nothing. Less malicious than a windbag, but more annoying.
+ Straight to the Tower
The Tower of London has long functioned as both the royal mint (and keeping place of the Crown Jewels) and a political prison. It is a very bad place to go if you're an enemy to those in power.
+ Annie Cobbie
A real and well-known/popular sex worker during the Renaissance. The "tawny moore with the soft skin", mentioned as such in historical records and in our play. "Anne was earning over four times as much as the other women" and "we can deduce that Anne Cobbie was a sought-after prostitute...[maybe] of North African heritage or possibly of mixed heritage."
+ But Soft!
Wait a minute!
+ Drag in 17th Century London
Drag was not uncommon in this time, both on the stages and on the streets (though often punished in the latter case). It was considered with suspicion, but allowed onstage prior to the Interregnum. Some evidence suggests that cross-dressing in regular life/on the streets may have been a more dominant behavior of women, for a variety of purposes mostly related to increased perception of personal freedom to navigate society. This came at a cost.
